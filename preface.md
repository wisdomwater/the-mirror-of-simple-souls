## Preface

I first came across the work of Marguerite Porete while searching for something quite simple: a book that spoke clearly about the love of God and how one might encounter it. Many spiritual writings describe devotion, discipline, or moral improvement. But I was looking for something deeper—something that spoke about the experience of divine love itself. In that search I stumbled upon *The Mirror of Simple Souls*, a medieval work that at once felt luminous and mysterious. Its language was dense, its images ancient, yet behind the words there seemed to be a startlingly beautiful message: that the soul can become so fully surrendered to divine love that nothing remains but love itself.

Marguerite Porete lived in the late 13th and early 14th centuries in what is now Belgium or northern France. She was likely associated with the Beguines, a movement of laywomen devoted to prayer and service who lived outside formal monastic structures. These women pursued deep spiritual lives while remaining within the rhythms of ordinary society. Marguerite’s writing reflects this contemplative spirit. In *The Mirror of Simple Souls*, she describes the journey of the soul beyond striving, beyond the anxious effort to prove one’s holiness, into a freedom where love alone governs the heart. For Marguerite, the highest spiritual life was not one of constant spiritual labor but one of surrender—where the soul becomes so united with divine love that its own will rests quietly within God’s will.

Such ideas, however, were controversial in her time. Some church authorities feared that her teachings diminished the importance of moral effort, spiritual discipline, and obedience to the structures of the church. Her book was condemned, and Marguerite was ordered to stop circulating it. She refused. She believed the message entrusted to her was too important to silence. After a long ecclesiastical trial, Marguerite Porete was declared a heretic and was burned at the stake in Paris in 1310. Yet her book survived. Copied and translated in secret, it continued to circulate among seekers who recognized in it a profound vision of divine love.

*The Mirror of Simple Souls* is both simple and difficult. Its message is simple: love already holds us completely. The soul does not need to manufacture holiness or force its way toward God. Instead, it is invited to release its anxious striving and rest in the love that has always surrounded it. Yet this simplicity can be surprisingly hard for us to receive. Our culture teaches us to prove ourselves, to perform, to strive for excellence in every area of life. Even in spiritual matters we often believe we must become better, more disciplined, more worthy before we can approach God. Marguerite gently turns this assumption upside down. She suggests that the very striving meant to reach God can sometimes obscure the love that is already present.

This little book is an attempt to make Marguerite’s vision more accessible for modern readers. The original text is beautiful but written in dense medieval language that can be difficult to follow. Here it has been retold as one hundred short daily reflections. Each reading is meant to be taken slowly—more like a quiet conversation than a lesson to master.

If you choose to read these pages, you may find that the deepest invitation is not to understand every idea, but simply to linger. To allow the words to soften the heart. To let the deeper part of the soul remember something it may have forgotten: that before we strive, before we prove, before we become anything at all—we are already held in love.

Carried by His embrace.

Michael
